Setup of the Environement

Forward Kinematics and Velocity Update

This simulation models soap bubble films as a dynamic mesh evolving under surface tension forces. The key insight is that the three well-known geometric perspectives (mean curvature, Laplacian smoothing, and the functional derivative of the area functional) are mathematically equivalent under this context: The mean curvature flow guide how the surface shrink and smooth out, mirroring how surface tension minimizes film's surface area. Computationally, this behavior can be modeled using the cotangent-weighted Laplacian, which applies smoothing forces based on the shape of the mesh. Mathematically, these forces also come from the gradient of the surface area, with cotangent weights appearing through proper energy discretization.

To simluate film movement over time, we uses forward kinematics step that updates vertex positions from their velocities and computes new velocities from Laplacian-derived forces. This implementation requires three main components: a mass matrix \( M \), a Laplacian matrix \( L \), and a position matrix \( X \).

Let \( n \) denote the number of vertices the system have. Each vertex i has position \( \mathbf{p}_i \) and velocity \( \mathbf{v}_i \).

Mass Matrix

We compute a diagonal inverse mass matrix \( \mathbf{M}^{-1} \) using barycentric area distribution. For a triangular face with vertices i,j,k, the area contribution is

\[ \text{share} = \big\|(\mathbf{p}_j - \mathbf{p}_i) \times (\mathbf{p}_k - \mathbf{p}_i)\big\|. \]

Each vertex's mass is computed from the sum of one-third of the area of its incident triangles.The inverse mass matrix is computed as:

\[ M_{ii}^{-1} = \frac{6}{\max(\text{mass}_i, \varepsilon)} \]

where \( \text{mass}_i \) is the sum of the areas of adjacent triangles. The factor of 6 accounts for barycentric weighting: each triangle contributes \( \frac{1}{3} \) of its area to each vertex, and the area computation does not account the usual \( \frac{1}{2} \) factor, hence resulting in an overestimation by a factor of 6.

Laplacian Matrix

The Laplacian \( \mathbf{L} \) computes smoothing forces using cotangent weights for each edge.

Cotangent weight for edge \((i, j)\) calculation where \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) are the angles opposite the edge in the two adjacent triangles.:

\[ w_{ij} = \frac{1}{2} \left( \cot \alpha + \cot \beta \right)\]

Individual weights then are inserted symmetrically and diagonals ensure row sums are zero:

\[ L_{ii} = -\sum_{j \in \mathcal{N}(i)} w_{ij}. \]

Velocity and Position Update

We compute accelerations from the Laplacian acting on the position matrix \( \mathbf{X} \) and integrate velocities explicitly. Using timestep \( \Delta t \):

\[ \mathbf{a} = \mathbf{M}^{-1} \mathbf{L} \mathbf{X} \]
\[ \mathbf{V}(t + \Delta t) = \mathbf{V}(t) + \Delta t \, \mathbf{a} \]
\[ \mathbf{X}(t + \Delta t) = \mathbf{X}(t) + \Delta t \, \mathbf{V}(t + \Delta t) \]

In this explicit Euler process, velocity is updated based on the current acceleration, and the position is then integrated forward using the updated velocity.

To maintain physically realistic bubble behavior, we enforce volume conservation through correction step. After each position update, we compute the current bubble volume and apply a uniform scaling about the center of mass:

\[ \text{scale} = \left( \frac{V_{\text{target}}}{V_{\text{current}}} \right)^{1/3} \]

The correction process involves:

  1. Compute the bubble's center of mass: \[ \mathbf{c} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \mathbf{p}_i \]
  2. Scale all vertices uniformly about c: \[ \mathbf{p}_i^{\text{new}} = \mathbf{c} + \text{scale} \cdot (\mathbf{p}_i - \mathbf{c}) \]

This correction preserves the bubble's shape while maintains the target bubble volume. It runs everytime after each position update, but before any topology changes (remeshing).

Isotropic Remeshing with Velocity Update

Forward kinematics steps could result in mesh irregularities, especially near poles where vertices may clusters. This happens because:

  • Pole Divergence: Vertices near poles experience higher curvature forces, causing them to accelerate faster and more clustered.
  • Edge Length Disparity: Some edges stretch too much (> 4L/3), while others shrink (< 4L/5), leading to poor mesh quality.

To restore mesh uniformity, we apply isotropic remeshing, which adjusts edge lengths and redistributes vertices to maintain consistent triangle shapes and sizes.

1. Tangential Smoothing

Smooths the mesh while preserving volume by moving vertices tangentially to avoid normal displacement. The position update for each vertex is computed as:

\[ \mathbf{p}_i^{\text{new}} = \mathbf{p}_i + \underbrace{(\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{n}_i \mathbf{n}_i^T)}_{\text{Tangent projection}} \left( \mathbf{p}_{\text{centroid}} - \mathbf{p}_i \right) \cdot w(d_i) \cdot \Delta t \]

where:

  • \(\mathbf{n}_i\) is the vertex normal
  • \(\ I - \mathbf{n}_i \mathbf{n}_i^T \) is the projection matrix that removes the normal component, equivalent to:
    Vector3D tangentialUpdate = update - normal * normalComponent
  • \(w(d_i) = \min(0.5, \max(0.01, 6/d_i))\) is the degree-adaptive weight
  • Velocity damping factor of 0.95 prevents oscillation

After applying the tangential update, the vertex velocity is recalculated based on the actual position change over the timestep:

\[ \mathbf{v}_i = \frac{(\mathbf{p}_{\text{new},i} - \mathbf{p}_{\text{old},i}) \cdot \text{damping}}{\Delta t} \]

2. Edge Operations

Edge Splitting

Purpose: Splits edges longer than 4L/3 to improve mesh uniformity.

Steps:

  1. Identify Long Edges (e->length() > threshold).
  2. Split Edge:
    Creates a new vertex at the midpoint using splitEdge(e) from HalfEdgeMesh.
    VertexIter newVert = splitEdge(e); newVert->position = 0.5 * (v0->position + v1->position);
  3. Interpolate Velocity (mass-weighted):
    The new vertex's velocity is computed as a mass-weighted average of the original edge vertices:
    V.row(new_idx) = (m0 * V.row(i0) + m1 * V.row(i1)) / (m0 + m1);
    where m0 and m1 are the masses of the original vertices.
  4. Update Mass (Minv(new_idx) = 1.0 / (m0 + m1)).

(B) Collapsing Short Edges (collapseShortEdges)

Purpose: Collapses edges shorter than 4L/5 to remove degeneracies.

Steps:

  1. Identify Short Edges (e->length() < threshold).
  2. Collapse Edge:
    Merges two vertices into one at the midpoint using collapseEdge(e) from HalfEdgeMesh.
    VertexIter newVert = collapseEdge(e); newVert->position = 0.5 * (v0->position + v1->position);
  3. Interpolate Velocity (mass-weighted):
    The new vertex's velocity preserves momentum:
    Vector3D newVel = (v0mass * v0Vel + v1mass * v1Vel) / (v0mass + v1mass);
    This ensures conservation of linear momentum during the collapse operation.
  4. Update Affected Edges:
    Recomputes lengths and reinserts into priority queue for further processing.

(C) Edge Flipping (flipEdgesForRegularDegree)

Purpose: Improves vertex connectivity by flipping edges to achieve more regular degree (6 neighbors for most vertices).

Note: While not shown in the provided code, edge flipping would typically use flipEdge(e) from HalfEdgeMesh and would similarly need to handle velocity updates for affected vertices.

3. Full Remeshing Pipeline (regularizeMesh)

Purpose: Combines splitting, collapsing, and smoothing for stable mesh evolution.

Algorithm:

  1. Compute Mean Edge Length (calculateMeanEdgeLength()).
  2. Iterative Remeshing (outerIterations = 5):
    • Collapse Short Edges (< 4L/5)
    • Split Long Edges (> 4L/3)
    • Tangential Smoothing (5 steps) to relax vertex positions
  3. Update Topology (topologyUpdated = true)

Key Benefits and Results

  • Prevents Pole Divergence: Tangential smoothing avoids normal displacement, keeping curvature stable.
  • Maintains Uniformity: Splitting/collapsing ensures edges stay close to the target length (L).
  • Preserves Physics: Mass-weighted velocity updates maintain energy conservation.

Summary

The remeshing system effectively maintains mesh quality during dynamic simulations by combining three key operations: tangential smoothing for stable deformation, edge splitting/collapsing for uniform triangle distribution, and proper velocity interpolation to preserve physical properties. By using mass-weighted velocity updates during topological changes (via splitEdge and collapseEdge from HalfEdgeMesh), the implementation ensures momentum conservation while preventing the artificial energy introduction that could lead to instability. The result is a robust system that prevents mesh degeneration (like pole divergence) while maintaining physically plausible motion - no more crashing from degenerate geometry or unstable simulations. The careful balance between geometric operations (splitting/collapsing) and physical quantities (velocity/mass interpolation) creates a stable feedback loop where the mesh quality supports accurate physics, and the physics informs appropriate remeshing.

Delta BSDF Sampling

To ensure our bubble bsdf rendering returns the correct color, we construct an RGB wavelength channel and use the helper function we just implemented to obtain the reflectance and transmittance for each RGB wavelength. We then use sRGB weights to calculate the reflected intensity \(w_R\) and transmitted intensity \(w_T\) perceived by the human eye. We then calculate the light reflection probability \(p_R = w_R / (w_T + w_R)\) and use it to determine whether the sample returns reflected or transmitted light.

  • Reflection geometry: perfect mirror (local reflection). \(f_{refl} = ( R_{rgb} · p_R) / |\cos \theta_i|\).
  • Transmission geometry: for a single-sided thin film, approximate as straight-through (local \(\omega_i = −\omega_o\)).  \(f_{trans} = ( T_{rgb} · p_T ) / |\cos \theta_i|\).

Skybox

To enhance visual richness and avoid a monotonous background, we implemented a skybox so that bubbles could reflect and refract more varied and realistic colors instead of appearing in a blank scene. We loaded six images, each mapped to one face of the cube, forming a complete surrounding environment. In the view matrix, we set \(v(0,3) = v(1,3) = v(2,3) = 0\) to remove translation while preserving rotation, ensuring that the cube remains stationary relative to the camera and appears infinitely distant.

For path tracing, we created a PathtracingSkybox::sample method that takes a normalized direction vector as input, determines which cube face this direction intersects, converts it to 2D texture coordinates, and returns the corresponding pixel color from that face. This method enabling the path tracer to return accurate background colors when rays miss all geometry.
